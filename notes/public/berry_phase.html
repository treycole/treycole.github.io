Berry Phase

The general definition of the Berry phase is a phase angle (from 0 to 2π) that a complex vector acquires as it is transported through a path in its complex vector space. The idea was popularized by Sir Michael Berry in 1984.

To demonstrate the Berry phase, we consider some state in Hilbert space (e.g. the ground state) of some system parameterized by a continuously varying parameter (e.g magnetic field, nuclear coordinates etc.). We then vary the parameter in a closed path, such that the system returns to where it started.

Adiabatic Approximation

If the parameters very “slowly” with time, then energy eigenvalues should just follow the change in parameters. The eigenvectors will acquire an additional geometrical phase associated with the change in parameter.

Discrete formulation

Consider N vectors ∣u0​⟩ to ∣uN−1​⟩ that describe the ground state of a system at each discrete step in parameter space, where the last state ∣uN​⟩ is identified with ∣u0​⟩. The Berry phase is defined as

ϕ=−Im ln[⟨u0​∣u1​⟩⟨u1​∣u2​⟩⋯⟨uN−1​∣u0​⟩]

The reason for the imaginary natural log is that for some complex number z=∣z∣eiϕ, Im lnz=ϕ , so we only retain the information about its phase. This definition chooses a branch of Im ln z such that −π<ϕ≤π. The minus sign is conventional. This formulation has the benefit that it is gauge independent.

Parallel-Transport Gauge

We are free to choose any gauge we like for each of the ∣uj​⟩ . We can define new gauges s.t. ∣uˉ0​⟩=∣u0​⟩ , then choose the phase of ∣u1​⟩ such that ⟨uˉ0​∣uˉ1​⟩ is real and positive. If we propagate this down the line of states, then

Im ln⟨uˉj​∣uˉj+1​⟩=0

This generates what is known as the parallel transport gauge. In this gauge choice,∣uˉ0​⟩ is no longer equivalent to ∣uˉN​⟩, and they differ by exactly the Berry phase

ϕ=Im ln⟨uˉN​∣uˉ0​⟩

The phase difference of the states are exactly zero until an abrupt discontinuity at the end of the loop.
